In Determined: A Science of Life Without Free Will, Robert Sapolsky makes a sweeping, forceful argument against the existence of free will, drawing heavily from neuroscience, psychology, evolutionary biology, and philosophy. Regarding emergence and convergence, he takes a strong stance that further buttresses his deterministic worldview: neither of these phenomena salvages the idea of free will.

On Emergence


Sapolsky explicitly rejects the notion that emergent properties create new forms of causality that could account for free will. Emergence, in this context, is the idea that complex systems (like brains) exhibit properties that are not obviously reducible to their individual parts. Some theorists have speculated that consciousness or decision-making might be emergent in such a way that they transcend physical causality.

Sapolsky calls this a category error—a kind of mystical hand-waving dressed up in scientific language. He argues that just because a system behaves in complex ways doesn’t mean it escapes causal determinism. Emergent properties, for Sapolsky, are still fully the result of prior causes; they don’t introduce magical new inputs. As he quips, emergence is just complexity we haven't yet unraveled.

He compares this to watching a murmuration of starlings or a traffic jam: the aggregate behavior might be unpredictable or dazzling, but every element still follows deterministic rules. That doesn’t mean there’s a ghost in the machine—it means the machine is more complicated than we thought.

On Convergence


Sapolsky uses convergent evolution as a counterexample to randomness or freedom. He argues that convergence—when different evolutionary lineages arrive at similar solutions (e.g., camera-like eyes in both octopi and vertebrates)—actually demonstrates the inevitability of certain outcomes, not the freedom to choose among many equally plausible paths.

So instead of undermining determinism, convergence supports it: it shows that when conditions are similar enough, the same outcomes will recur. If you reran the tape of life, he argues, you wouldn’t get radically different results—you’d get the same constrained solutions again and again. This perspective is deeply hostile to any libertarian or indeterministic model of free will.

Verdict


For Sapolsky, emergence is not magic and convergence is not choice. Both are misunderstood or misapplied by those grasping to preserve some form of metaphysical wiggle room for human freedom. His deterministic model insists that every thought, impulse, and decision is traceable—either in principle or in practice—to prior causes, including genes, neurotransmitters, developmental environments, social contexts, and even bacteria in your gut.

If there's a central message of Determined, it’s this: we are consequence, not cause.
