The Logic of Bequest

This is where the bequest becomes strategic. A dying pope has little material power, but enormous symbolic leverage. By shaping the college in his image‚Äîvia cardinal appointments, reforms to voting procedures, or doctrinal emphasis‚Äîhe sets the conditions of possibility. What looks like mere gesture is, in fact, a long game of institutional reproduction. Popes, like CEOs, can‚Äôt dictate their successor, but they can rig the board. Pattern and Prophecy

And what of the uncanny timing? Catholicism, with its millennia-deep grooves of ritual and hierarchy, is a perfect staging ground for narrative recursion. It rewards the slow burn, the symbolic overture, the long memory. Conclave is not predictive in the way a weather model is; it is predictive the way Greek tragedy is. It lays out the inevitabilities: the palace intrigue, the frailty masked by liturgy, the tension between spiritual continuity and bureaucratic entropy. It teaches us to expect the shockingly familiar.[5]

In the end, the succession of Leo XIV is not simply a media story or a religious event; it is a case study in institutional design. Strategic bequests, as theorized in economics, explain why certain agents transfer assets not purely out of altruism, but to incentivize future behavior. In the corporate world, this might mean stock options or succession bonuses. In the Vatican, it means appointing bishops and cardinals who will steer the barque of Peter along the intended theological course. Succession as Sacrament

The real genius of Conclave is not its script but its structure. The film‚Äîand now history‚Äîreminds us that even in the age of liberal cynicism, the Vatican remains one of the few institutions where succession is not merely personnel but prophecy.
